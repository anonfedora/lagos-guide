Navigating Lagos

Lagos traffic is legendary. A 10 km trip can take 20 minutes or 2 hours depending on the time. Ride-hailing apps are your best friend, but always have a backup plan.

Rule number one

Respect the traffic

Peak hours run roughly 7 to 10am and 4 to 8pm. The Third Mainland Bridge and Lekki-Epe Expressway can gridlock completely. Always build in a buffer and check traffic apps before heading out.

Reading the tags

What the labels mean

  • Vegetarian contains no meat, fish, or egg, but may contain dairy. Vegan has no animal products at all. Dairy-free avoids milk, butter, cream, and cheese.
  • "On request" means the dish can be made that way if you ask clearly, since the default may include meat stock or palm oil.
  • Halal meat follows Islamic slaughter, which is the standard for most meat in Nigeria.
  • For non-veg places the meat type is named: goat, chicken, beef, fish, seafood, or assorted meats.
Spice, dairy and allergies

Say it clearly and you will be fine

  • Spice: say "less spicy" or "no pepper." Nigerian medium can be quite hot. Yogurt or plantain helps cool the burn.
  • Dairy: Nigerian cuisine is naturally dairy-free in many dishes, but some restaurants add butter or cream. Specify if you need dairy-free.
  • Allergies: Flag serious allergies clearly. Seafood and groundnut (peanut) are common in Nigerian cuisine.

💧Stay healthy

Water and health

The fastest way to ruin your trip is a stomach bug. A little caution with water and food keeps you healthy.

Read this first

Do not drink the tap water

Lagos tap water is not safe for visitors to drink. Traveller's diarrhoea is the single most common thing that ruins a trip. Stick to sealed bottled or properly filtered water for your entire stay, including for brushing your teeth.

Safe to drink

Green light
  • Sealed bottled water with an intact seal (Eva, Nestle Pure Life, Ragolis). Hotels usually provide it free.
  • Boiled or filtered water from your hotel.
  • Hot tea and coffee, since boiling makes them safe.
  • Sealed cans and bottles, and fresh coconut water from the shell.

Avoid

Red light
  • Tap water, including for brushing teeth. Use bottled.
  • Ice from unknown sources and street stalls.
  • Raw salads and pre-cut fruit washed in tap water at carts.
  • Refilled "bottled" water. Always check the seal cracks when you open it.

Pack a tummy kit

Before you fly

Bring ORS rehydration salts, an antidiarrhoeal such as loperamide, and ask your doctor about a standby antibiotic for traveller's diarrhoea. Hand sanitiser and your own refillable bottle keep you in the game. Eat hot, freshly cooked food, and pick busy stalls with high turnover over empty ones.

Pharmacies and hospitals

If things go wrong

Pharmacies (called chemists) are on every corner; chains like HealthPlus and Medplus run late. For anything serious, Lagos has excellent private hospitals.

Lagoon Hospital, VI ↗ Reddington, VI ↗ Eko Hospital, Lekki ↗
Before you travel

Vaccines, insurance and prescriptions

Check routine vaccines plus Hepatitis A, Typhoid, and Yellow Fever (required if arriving from yellow fever endemic countries) with a travel clinic a month ahead. Buy travel and medical insurance that covers Nigeria, and carry it on your phone. Bring enough of any prescription medicine in its original packaging, plus a copy of the prescription. Mosquito repellent is worth packing year-round.

📶Connectivity and money

SIM, data and paying

Lagos runs on mobile data and cash. Get connected and understand payments on day one, and everything else gets easier.

Getting a SIM

What and how
  • Easiest is an eSIM bought before you fly. Activate on landing, no paperwork. Best for most international attendees. Try Airalo ↗ or Holafly ↗.
  • Best local networks are MTN ↗ and Airtel ↗ for coverage and 4G / 5G. Glo and 9mobile are alternatives. Tourist plans run roughly ₦5,000 to 15,000 for the trip.
  • Physical SIM on arrival: buy at the official MTN or Airtel counter in the airport arrivals hall. Bring your passport, visa, and a passport photo. Activation can take a few hours.
  • ID is mandatory (KYC). Use official stores, not street vendors, so it activates reliably.
  • Nigerian residents just need extra data for the event crowd load.

Money and payments

Cash, cards, transfers
  • Cash is still king. Many small businesses, markets, and transport options only accept cash. Carry naira in small denominations.
  • Bank transfers are popular via apps like OPay ↗, Kuda ↗, and traditional bank apps. Requires Nigerian bank account.
  • Cards work at hotels, malls, and nicer restaurants. Tell your bank you are travelling and carry a backup card. Visa and Mastercard are widely accepted.
  • ATMs are everywhere; use ones attached to banks, withdraw by day, and cover the keypad. Daily withdrawal limits apply.
  • Currency is the Nigerian Naira (NGN). Rates fluctuate, so check the live rate ↗ before you budget.
Power and plugs

Stay charged

Nigeria uses 230V, 50Hz, with Type D and G sockets. Bring a universal adapter; the British-style Type G is common. Pack a power bank, because long days plus maps and ride apps drain a phone fast. Hotels have charging, but power outages are common, so don't count on constant electricity.

🛠️For builders

Tech and coworking

Lagos has a growing tech ecosystem with active developer communities. A few local realities are worth knowing before you arrive.

Crypto regulation

Know the rules

Nigeria's Central Bank has restrictions on crypto transactions through banks, but peer-to-peer trading remains active. This mainly matters if you transact through Nigerian exchanges or as a resident. Stay informed on current regulations before doing anything onchain for business here.

Community and meetups

Plug in

Lagos has a vibrant grassroots tech scene with groups like Web3 Lagos, Blockchain Nigeria, and various developer meetups. Expect side events, hacker houses, and meetups during major conferences. Watch local communities for the schedule.

Working from the city

Coworking and cafes

Victoria Island, Ikoyi, and Lekki have coworking spaces and laptop-friendly cafes. Hotels in VI and Ikoyi typically have business centers. Generator backup is essential due to power outages.

🛡️Stay safe and smart

Street smarts

Lagos is a vibrant but complex megacity. Basic awareness goes a long way. Most visitors have trouble-free stays by following simple precautions.

The legal lines, do not cross them

Smoking, drinking and drugs

  • Drugs, including cannabis: all recreational drugs are illegal in Nigeria under the NDLEA Act. Penalties are severe, including jail even for small amounts. Simply do not.
  • Smoking is legal for adults but banned in most public places. Use designated smoking areas only.
  • Alcohol: legal in licensed bars, restaurants, and hotels. The legal drinking age is 18. Drinking in public is illegal. Buy only from licensed shops or venues.

When in doubt, check your government's Nigeria travel advisory ↗.

Getting around like a local

Daily moves
  • Default to app cabs (Uber / Bolt) for transparent, tracked, shareable rides, especially at night.
  • In street taxis, agree the fare before you start. No meters.
  • Download Google Maps offline for Lagos in case of poor data coverage.
  • Carry small cash for vendors and tips, and keep your hotel address saved and on paper.
  • Cross roads carefully; traffic does not always stop. Use pedestrian bridges where they exist.
  • Avoid walking alone at night in unfamiliar areas. Stick to well-lit, busy streets.

Personal safety

Day-to-day
  • Keep valuables zipped and in front in crowds, markets, and public transport. Petty pickpocketing is the main risk.
  • Do not flash expensive gear or large amounts of cash. Take your event badge off in public.
  • Politely ignore touts and unsolicited offers or anyone steering you to a specific shop.
  • Women travellers: prefer app cabs at night, dress modestly in conservative areas, and be cautious at night.
  • Keep digital and paper copies of your passport, visa, and ticket.
  • Buy travel and medical insurance before you fly, and save your embassy's number.

💡Other things to keep in mind

Good to know

The small stuff that makes a trip smooth.

Visa and entry

e-Visa ↗

Most foreign nationals need a visa. The official e-Visa is available for many countries. Apply on the government portal weeks ahead. Check requirements for your nationality.

Weather

Tropical

Warm and humid year-round, roughly 25 to 32°C (77 to 90°F). Pack light breathable clothes, sunscreen, and rain gear for unexpected showers.

Time zone

WAT

West Africa Time (WAT) is UTC plus 1, with no daylight saving. Plan calls home accordingly.

Language

English works

English is the official language and widely spoken in business. Pidgin English is common locally. A few Nigerian phrases go far.

Dress code

Casual to smart

Casual is fine for the city. Cover shoulders and knees at religious sites, carry a scarf, and remove shoes at mosques and many homes.

Tipping

Appreciated

Not mandatory but welcome: about 5 to 10% at restaurants (check if service charge is already added), round up for cabs, and ₦200 to 500 for hotel help.

Etiquette

Small courtesies

Use your right hand for eating and giving. Ask before photographing people. Bargaining is normal in markets, not in fixed-price shops and restaurants.

Restrooms

Be prepared

Use hotels, malls, cafes, and restaurants. Carry tissue and hand sanitiser, since many public toilets lack paper.

Air quality

Live AQI ↗

Air quality can vary due to traffic and generators. If you are sensitive, pack a mask and check the live index.
